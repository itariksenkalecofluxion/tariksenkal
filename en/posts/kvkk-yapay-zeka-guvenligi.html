Legal data in the age of the KVKK: privacy and security in artificial intelligence

TŞ
İsmail Tarık Şenkal
EcoFluxion · Co-Founder
· 9 min read

A lawyer's case file is not an ordinary pile of data. It may contain the fragile details of a divorce, a draft contract that will shape a company's future, or a defense strategy not yet disclosed to anyone. Artificial intelligence has reached the power to process such data in seconds; but the real question is this: when we hand that data to a machine, are we also handing over our client's secret?

At EcoFluxion, while building artificial intelligence for the legal field, we have never relegated this question to second place. Because in law, data is the most sensitive asset, and protecting it is not a technical preference but a professional and legal obligation. In this article, from client confidentiality to the concrete obligations of the KVKK, from encryption to the principle that "your data must not be used to train the model," we set out the framework for using legal data safely in the age of artificial intelligence.

Why is data the most sensitive asset in law?

In most sectors, "data security" is a matter of reputation and compliance. In law, it goes far beyond that: a data leak can lose a case, upend a person's life, and even put a lawyer's career at risk. A client entrusts their most intimate information to their lawyer within a relationship of trust. The return on that trust is not merely a good defense; it is an absolute commitment that the information will remain a secret.

Digitalization has made this equation both easier and more dangerous. The file that once sat in a locked cabinet now travels through the cloud, email, messaging apps, and, increasingly, artificial intelligence tools. Every new digital stop means a new door for a leak. When artificial intelligence is added to this chain, the question is no longer "should we use the data?" but "how do we use it safely?"

For a lawyer, the greatest technological risk is not failing to use artificial intelligence; it is using it blindly.

Attorney-client confidentiality: an indefinite, non-transferable burden

Under Turkish law, a lawyer's duty of confidentiality is not a courtesy rule but a legal obligation. Article 36 of the Attorneys' Act prohibits lawyers from disclosing anything they learn in the course of their duties. Three features of this obligation become critical when artificial intelligence is involved:

  • Its scope is broad. It covers not only the client's oral statements, but documents, correspondence, and all information learned through the case.
  • It is indefinite. The secret does not disappear when the case ends, or even when the relationship with the client is over; the obligation lasts a lifetime.
  • It extends to support staff. From the secretary to the trainee, everyone working in the office is within this circle of confidentiality.

This is precisely where the problem begins. When you paste a client document into a carelessly chosen artificial intelligence tool and say "summarize this," you may have taken that data outside the firm's circle of confidentiality. If the tool stores that data, shares it with third parties, or uses it to train its own model, this is not a technical detail; it is a potential breach of confidentiality. The very logic that brings support staff into the circle of confidentiality requires you to evaluate the technological tools you use with the same rigor.

The core principles of the KVKK and data security

Attorney-client confidentiality stems from professional law; the KVKK, by contrast, is a general framework binding on everyone. Law No. 6698 on the Protection of Personal Data imposes concrete obligations on anyone who processes personal data, and a law firm is one of the most data-intensive personal-data processors there is.

Article 4: the core principles of processing

Article 4 of the Act sets out the anchors that every data-processing activity must comply with. When using artificial intelligence, these principles should be read as a checklist:

  • Compliance with the law and with good-faith rules: processing must not involve a use that would surprise the data subject or exceed their expectations.
  • Being accurate and up to date: a model fed with inaccurate data produces inaccurate results.
  • Specific, explicit, and legitimate purpose: you must define from the outset why you are processing the data.
  • Purpose limitation and proportionality (data minimization): this is perhaps the most vital principle for artificial intelligence. If only the legal reasoning of a pleading is enough for a summary, there is no need whatsoever to upload the client's ID number, address, and every personal detail into the tool.
  • Storage limitation: data must not be retained longer than necessary.
Tip: Before handing a document to artificial intelligence, ask yourself: "Is all of this data really necessary for this task?" More often than not, the answer is "no." Masking personal details is the most practical way to apply the minimization principle.

Article 12: the data security obligation

Article 12 of the KVKK imposes on the data controller the duty to take the technical and administrative measures necessary to ensure an appropriate level of security. The guidelines published by the Board give concrete form to this abstract wording: it recommends that data at rest and in backups be encrypted with strong algorithms (for example, AES-256), and that encryption keys be stored in secure environments such as an HSM. In practice, this makes encryption all but mandatory.

And then there is the harder side: if a data breach occurs, the Personal Data Protection Board must be notified within 72 hours of the breach being detected. Late notification leads to an administrative fine. With the 2024 regulations, the penalties relating to breach- and standard-contract-notification obligations have reached substantial amounts that are updated each year by the revaluation rate.

Explicit consent and disclosure

The KVKK defines explicit consent as "consent relating to a specific subject, based on being informed, and expressed by free will." In other words, an "okay" obtained without clearly explaining to the client that their data may be processed in AI-assisted workflows is legally weak. Moreover, the Board requires the explicit-consent text and the disclosure text to be drafted separately; combining the two into a single paragraph is not sound practice.

Cross-border transfer: where is your data going?

This is the most critical point that most lawyers overlook. Uploading a client document "for analysis" to an artificial intelligence tool most often means transferring that data to servers abroad (usually in the United States). From the KVKK's perspective, this is not an ordinary operation but a "cross-border data transfer," and it has its own rules.

The good news is that the 2024 reform brought a clearer footing on this matter. The standard contracts adopted by the Regulation dated 10.07.2024 and the Board decision dated 04.06.2024 provide a legal basis for transfer under certain conditions. But here, too, there is an obligation: the Authority must be notified within five business days of signing the standard contract. In other words, behind the convenience of "I opened the tool, I uploaded the document" lies a compliance process that, if neglected, can turn into a penalty.

This shows why, when choosing an artificial intelligence tool, it is vital to ask where the data is processed and where it is stored. When building our infrastructure at EcoFluxion on Google Cloud and placing KVKK compliance at the heart of the design, we aimed to answer precisely these questions from the very beginning.

The critical principle: your data must not train the model

When it comes to legal data, this is the single most critical principle: the data you provide must never be used to train the tool's artificial intelligence model. Because when a model is trained on a piece of data, that data can leak into the model's "memory" and, in theory, surface in front of another user. For client confidentiality, this is an unacceptable scenario.

Fortunately, serious enterprise providers have come to accept this reality. A few concrete examples:

  • OpenAI has committed that, as of March 1, 2023, it does not use data received via the API (absent an explicit opt-in) to train models; inputs and outputs are deleted by default after 30 days, and a "Zero Data Retention" (ZDR) option is offered for eligible enterprise use.
  • Anthropic does not, by default, use customer data to train models on its commercial and enterprise tiers; as of September 14, 2025, it reduced the standard API log retention period from 30 days to 7 days and offers a ZDR option for eligible customers.

The distinction to watch for is this: these assurances generally apply to the enterprise and API tiers. The same protection often does not exist in the same tool's public, free consumer version. And this is where the most common mistake lawyers make is hidden: pasting a client document into a free chat window is not the same as making an API call protected by the right enterprise agreement.

The direction on the European side is the same. The EDPB's Opinion 28/2024, dated December 17, 2024, confirms that the core principles of the GDPR also apply to artificial intelligence models; it emphasizes that for a model to be considered "anonymous," both the likelihood of directly identifying individuals and the likelihood of extracting personal data back through queries must be insignificant. In short, saying "we use artificial intelligence" does not mean being exempt from data-protection obligations.

Technical safeguards: encryption and secure infrastructure

Principles matter; but what keeps them standing is the technical infrastructure. The fundamental safeguards to look for in a trustworthy legal AI solution are:

  • Encryption of data at rest and in transit. On mature cloud platforms such as Google Cloud, all stored data is automatically encrypted with AES-256; data in transit is protected with TLS. NIST recommends AES-256 for long-term use.
  • Key management. As much as the encryption itself, how the encryption keys are stored matters too. It is essential that keys be kept in managed, secure systems such as an HSM or KMS.
  • Access control and logging. Who accesses the data, and when, must be recorded; permissions should be limited according to the "need to know" principle.

When designing our products İçtiHub and MevzuatBot to work with the case law of the four high courts (the Court of Cassation (Yargıtay), the Council of State (Danıştay), the Constitutional Court, and the Regional Courts of Justice (BAM)), we saw security not as a feature bolted on afterward, but as the foundation of the architecture.

A real-world warning: hallucination and the loss of privilege

Privacy is not the only risk in legal AI. Two dangers are in fact intertwined: information leaking out, and information being fabricated.

The most famous example of the latter is Mata v. Avianca (S.D.N.Y., 2023), now a cautionary lesson in the legal world. The client's lawyers had submitted to the court fake case law fabricated by ChatGPT (that is, citations that did not actually exist). When Judge P. Kevin Castel detected the situation, he dismissed the case and fined the lawyers USD 5,000. This case strikingly shows that no legal output produced by artificial intelligence can be used without passing through human review.

On the privacy side, the American Bar Association's (ABA) Formal Opinion 512, dated July 29, 2024, contains a striking warning: public generative artificial intelligence systems may retain the data entered into them as training data. For this reason, entering privileged content into such a system may be deemed disclosure to a third party and lead to the loss of attorney-client privilege. Considered together with the duty of attorney-client confidentiality under Turkish law, the message is clear: choosing the wrong tool can give rise not only to a data leak, but to a professional loss of confidentiality.

Key takeaways

  • Attorney-client confidentiality is indefinite, broad, and extends to support staff; evaluate the artificial intelligence tool you use as part of that same circle.
  • The heart of Article 4 of the KVKK is data minimization: give the tool only the data genuinely needed for the task.
  • Uploading data to artificial intelligence is, in most cases, a cross-border transfer; do not neglect the standard contract and the notification obligation.
  • The most critical principle is that your data must not be used for model training. Do not confuse free consumer versions with enterprise/ZDR tiers.
  • AES-256 encryption, key management, and access logging are not a luxury but a minimum foundation.
  • No artificial intelligence output should be used without passing through human review; Mata v. Avianca showed the price of doing so.

A checklist for the law firm

Let us distill this entire framework into a practical list a lawyer can keep at the edge of their desk. Before processing any client data with artificial intelligence:

  1. Have you chosen the right tool? Are you using a solution with enterprise assurances, rather than a public, free chat window?
  2. Is the data kept out of model training? Does the provider give this commitment explicitly; is ZDR or short retention available?
  3. Are explicit consent and disclosure in place? Is the client aware that their data will be processed in AI-assisted workflows?
  4. Has minimization been applied? Did you provide only the necessary data; did you mask unnecessary personal details?
  5. Is the transfer encrypted and compliant? Is the data protected with AES-256/TLS, and does the cross-border transfer rest on a legal basis?
  6. Is there human review? Did a legal professional verify the output, and especially the case law and legislation citations, before it was submitted?

If I use artificial intelligence, am I transferring data abroad under the KVKK?

In most cases, yes. The data is generally processed on servers abroad. The standard contracts introduced by the 2024 reform provide a legal footing for this; however, there is an obligation to notify the Authority within five business days of signing the contract. For this reason, it is essential to know where the tool processes your data.

How can I tell whether my data is used for model training?

Look at the provider's privacy and enterprise terms. OpenAI commits that it has not used API data (absent an opt-in) for training since March 2023; Anthropic commits that, by default, it does not use customer data for training on its commercial tiers. Options such as Zero Data Retention (ZDR) provide additional assurance. Even so, these assurances are generally for the enterprise/API tiers, not for the free versions.

How do I protect myself from case law fabricated by artificial intelligence?

The only reliable way is human review. In the Mata v. Avianca case, the lawyers were fined because they submitted to the court fake decisions fabricated by artificial intelligence without verifying them. Part of the solution is also to use methods such as RAG that ground answers in real documents; but no technical method can replace a legal professional's final check.

Artificial intelligence is an extraordinary tool that accelerates legal practice. But in law, speed can never come before trust. Choosing the right tool, minimizing your data, using an encrypted and compliant infrastructure, and reviewing every output through the eyes of a legal professional: these are the steps that turn artificial intelligence from a risk into a genuine colleague.

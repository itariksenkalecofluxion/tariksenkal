Will AI replace lawyers?

TŞ
İsmail Tarık Şenkal
EcoFluxion · Co-Founder
· 9 min read

“Will AI replace lawyers?” has been the headline we have run into most often over the past three years. Our honest answer is this: it is somewhat the wrong question. A more useful one is — which tasks become automated, and which remain with humans? In this piece we proceed neither with fear nor with awe, but with the balanced picture drawn by the numbers, the court rulings, and the rules of the profession.

The wrong question, the right question

The question “will it replace them?” treats the profession as a single, indivisible block. Yet practicing law is the sum of dozens of very different tasks: there is the hours-long search through case law, but also looking a client in the eye and walking them through the worst-case scenario; there is catching the risky clause in a contract, but also sitting down at the table to negotiate with the other side. Some of these tasks are highly repetitive and formulaic; others demand reasoning, intuition, and responsibility.

That is why the right question is not about the future of “the lawyer” but about the future of “the tasks.” Industry leaders and academic sources are surprisingly aligned on this point: when AI automates the routine and shifts the lawyer toward higher-value work, productivity rises; but when it comes to judgment, strategy, and human skills, they see no substitution at all.

The real issue is not whether the machine will replace the lawyer, but which tasks the lawyer will hand over to the machine and which they will keep for themselves.

The real situation, in numbers

The most frequently cited figure comes from Goldman Sachs: generative AI could automate roughly 44% of legal tasks in the United States. This is a sentence that must be read carefully. What gets automated is not the profession but the tasks; moreover, “automatable” does not mean “can be done without a lawyer.”

Adoption is accelerating too: the share of legal professionals using generative AI in their work nearly doubled in a single year (from about 14% to 26%). By Thomson Reuters’ estimate, a lawyer’s annual time savings can reach 190 hours — that is weeks of working time. But perhaps the most striking data point is this: the labor market shows no substitution yet. In the U.S., the 2024 law graduates reached a record employment rate within ten months of graduation, and firm hiring increased year over year. In other words, the numbers do not bear out the “the robots put lawyers out of work” narrative.

The tasks AI is genuinely good at

Where AI shines is in high-volume, formulaic information work. These are exactly the areas we focused on when developing İçtiHub:

  • Legal research: bringing the relevant case law and legislation on a topic in front of you within minutes rather than hours.
  • Document review and e-discovery: scanning thousands of pages to flag relevant passages, risky wording, and contradictions.
  • Summarization: quickly extracting the essence of a long ruling or an expert report.
  • First draft: producing a starting text for a petition, contract, or legal opinion — solving the blank-page problem.
  • Contract review: checking standard clauses and catching missing or unusual provisions.

What these tasks have in common is this: the input is large, the pattern is clear, and the output will invariably be verified by a human. AI’s strength is speed and scope; its weakness is ultimate reliability. That very distinction is the subject of the next section.

The tasks that stay with humans

The other side of the list is no less clear than the first. These are the “un-machinable” core of a case file:

  • Strategy: which case to bring, which path to take, when to settle — these require context, experience, and risk appetite.
  • The trust relationship with the client: listening to a frightened, angry, or desperate person and giving them reassurance is not an algorithm’s job.
  • Negotiation: reading the person across the table, knowing when to press and when to pull back.
  • Representation in court and cross-examination: a wholly human performance that demands split-second reaction, persuasion, and command of the room.
  • Ethical judgment and conscientious reasoning: seeing the difference between what is legally possible and what is right.
  • Novel problems: blazing a trail in unprecedented situations that call for interpretation.

This last item is especially critical. Independent assessments show that even the best models struggle markedly with hard, previously unseen legal problems: they are strong on tasks that fit a pattern; but on high-stakes work that demands genuine reasoning and interpretation, they are still inadequate. The pivotal moments of justice often occur precisely in these places that “do not fit a pattern.”

The “augmented lawyer” thesis

So the picture is not an “either/or.” The real story is the value that emerges when human and AI work together. A controlled field experiment demonstrated this idea in the most concrete terms: when law students worked with well-designed AI tools, their productivity rose markedly — manyfold on some tasks.

But the second half of the same study is even more instructive than the first. Verifying the output still remained with the human; what is more, there was a serious difference between the tools. The tool working with a RAG approach, which grounds its answers in real documents, kept its error rate at the same low level as those using no AI at all. The model reasoning solely from its own “memory,” by contrast, delivered speed but produced fabrications. In short, the productivity gain is not free; it comes with the right architecture and human oversight.

Tip: When evaluating a legal AI, the first question to ask should not be “how fast?” but “what does it base its answer on?” The difference between systems grounded in a real source (RAG) and systems that merely make predictions is the whole of reliability.

The other side of the coin: hallucination and liability

We also need to talk openly about the risky side of this. Stanford’s RegLab research revealed that even leading commercial legal-research tools marketed with a “no hallucinations” claim produced fabrications on a significant share of queries — for some tools this rate approached one in three. So the “purpose-built legal tool” label is not, on its own, a guarantee.

The cost of this is not theoretical either. In the Mata v. Avianca case (2023), lawyers placed in their brief six fake precedents fabricated by ChatGPT; none of the rulings actually existed. The judge held the lawyers who skipped verification accountable and imposed a fine. Nor was this an isolated accident: in 2024–2025, new sanctions came on similar grounds — such as the fines imposed on firms in California and the sanction applied in Utah for a nonexistent precedent. The shared lesson is always the same: it is not the model that made the error but the lawyer who skipped verification who is held responsible.

Every sentence AI produces bears the signature of the lawyer who submits it to the court. And the responsibility of that signature cannot be delegated.

Professional responsibility and ethics

This is why the matter is not merely technical but, from start to finish, ethical. The American Bar Association’s (ABA) first formal ethics opinion on generative AI (Opinion 512, 2024) sets out the lawyer’s obligations clearly: competence, confidentiality, communication with the client, candor toward the tribunal, supervision, and reasonable fees. The essence of the opinion gathers into a single principle: relying uncritically on output without independent verification can in itself be a competence violation; the ultimate responsibility always remains with the lawyer.

It is at exactly this point that data security also comes into play. At EcoFluxion, when we built İçtiHub and MevzuatBot, we adopted this as a foundational principle: KVKK compliance, AES-256 encryption, and most importantly — user data is not used in model training. Because a lawyer’s case file is their professional secret itself; no tool that fails to protect it can be deemed “competent.”

The Turkish context

This debate is rapidly becoming institutionalized in Turkey as well. The Union of Turkish Bar Associations (TBB) established an “Artificial Intelligence and Law R&D Unit” and held the Artificial Intelligence and Advocacy Workshop in 2026. The TBB’s message aligns exactly with the framing of this piece: the legal profession involves reasoning, responsibility, and ethical assessment; no algorithm can take the place of these elements.

There is also a shared stance that puts this in sharper terms: a joint statement by 78 bar associations declared that “the right to a defense cannot be delegated to artificial intelligence” and stressed that justice is not a mathematical result obtained merely through data processing, but the product of human reasoning passed through the filter of conscience. The joint workshop report of the Istanbul, Ankara, and İzmir bar associations follows a similar line, positioning AI as a tool — not as a decision-maker.

Key takeaways

  • What gets automated is not “the lawyer” but the tasks — research, drafting, summarization, document scanning.
  • Strategy, negotiation, representation in court, ethical judgment, and ultimate responsibility stay with humans.
  • Even the most powerful model supports the “augmented lawyer”: AI provides the speed, the human does the verification.
  • Hallucination is a real risk; the penalties are always imposed on the lawyer who skipped verification.
  • The secret to reliability is grounding the answer in a real source (RAG) and protecting the data.
Will AI put lawyers out of work in the near future?

The data does not show this. In the U.S., the employment rate of law graduates has climbed to record levels, and firm hiring has increased. What gets automated is not the entire profession but the routine tasks within it. The lawyer’s role is not disappearing; it is shifting toward higher-value work.

Can I submit a petition prepared by AI to the court as is?

Absolutely not. Mata v. Avianca and the subsequent sanction cases came up for exactly this reason. Every citation, every precedent, and every figure must be verified by a human. Responsibility belongs to the lawyer; “the AI wrote it” is not an excuse.

Which AI tool should I trust in law?

The first criterion is reliability. Tools that ground their answers in real documents (RAG) produce far fewer errors than models that merely make predictions. Also look at data security: is your data used in model training, is it encrypted, is it KVKK-compliant?

What does “augmented lawyer” mean?

It means a lawyer who hands routine work over to AI and devotes the time gained to uniquely human tasks such as strategy, the client relationship, and reasoning. Not substitution but collaboration: the machine provides the speed, the human takes on the decision and the responsibility.

Conclusion: transformation, not substitution

Let us return to the start. Our answer to the question “Will AI replace lawyers?” is clear: no — but the lawyer who uses AI responsibly may replace the one who does not. Technology that takes over the routine does not put the lawyer out of work; it transforms them into a professional who can devote more time to the genuinely human and valuable part of the profession — to reasoning, to advocacy, to conscience.

At EcoFluxion (we were founded in 2024 at METU Technopark in Ankara), we develop İçtiHub and MevzuatBot with exactly this philosophy: tools that ground the case law of the four high courts (the Court of Cassation (Yargıtay), the Council of State (Danıştay), the Constitutional Court, and the Regional Courts of Justice (BAM)) in a real source — tools that do not take the lawyer’s place but work alongside them. We believe not in fear, but in transformation.

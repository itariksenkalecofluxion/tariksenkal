What is a large language model (LLM)?

TŞ
İsmail Tarık Şenkal
EcoFluxion · Co-Founder
· 8 min read

We have been hearing the abbreviation “LLM” everywhere over the past few years. It stands for Large Language Model. Tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude are all LLMs. But what exactly does this mean? Let me explain it in a way you will understand even if you have no technical background at all.

“An LLM is a prediction machine that has read an enormous amount of text and learned to answer the question ‘what should the next word be?’ incredibly well.”

In the simplest terms

Think of the word prediction on your phone's keyboard. When you type “Today the weather is very…”, it suggests “nice,” “hot,” or “cold.” An LLM does essentially the same thing — but because it learns by reading millions of books, articles, and web pages, its predictions become so accurate that it can fluently produce entire paragraphs, code, and even poems.

That is all there is to it. If you take the idea of “predict the next word” and do it at a large enough scale, the result is a system that can genuinely use language.

What does “large” mean?

Large in two senses:

  • A lot of data: The model is trained on an enormous amount of text.
  • A lot of parameters: Parameters are like the “tuning knobs” the model learns; there can be billions of them. The more knobs, the finer the tuning.
A rough analogy: Parameters are like the neural connections in the brain. As their number grows, the model's “intuition” gets stronger — but this requires serious computing power to train.

How is it trained?

  1. Pre-training: The model is shown mountains of text and learns to predict missing words. This is where it picks up the patterns of language.
  2. Fine-tuning: It is specialized for a particular domain (for example, Turkish law). MevzuatBot is exactly this.
  3. Alignment: Through human feedback, it is made to give more helpful and safer answers.

What are its limits?

LLMs are powerful, but not magical. Their best-known weakness: they can sometimes confidently make up a wrong answer (this is called “hallucination”). That is because the model predicts “the next word”; it is not programmed to “tell the truth.”

This is precisely why using an LLM on its own is risky in sensitive areas like law. The solution is to ground the model in real documents — this is called RAG, and it is the subject of the next post.

Why does it matter for us?

Because most powerful LLMs are trained predominantly on English text, they can fall short in Turkish. The work we do at EcoFluxion is exactly about closing this gap: training our own models that genuinely understand Turkish, and Turkish legal language in particular. I explained why this is an issue in the post “Why Turkish AI?”

In short: an LLM is a giant model that has learned to predict language extraordinarily well. It is tremendous when used correctly; but for accuracy, it needs to be anchored to real data.

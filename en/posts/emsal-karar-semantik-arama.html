Finding precedent by meaning: semantic search in law

TŞ
İsmail Tarık Şenkal
EcoFluxion · Co-Founder
· 8 min read

One of the most maddening moments for a lawyer is this: you are sure the precedent you need is in there somewhere, yet no matter which word you search by, it never comes up. "What if I typed the wrong term?" That small doubt is an invisible wall between you and the right reasoning, lost among millions of decisions. Semantic search exists precisely to tear that wall down.

Why is finding the right precedent so hard?

In law, the right decision usually runs through finding the right precedent. But this "finding" is far tougher than it looks. You face millions of decisions, with only limited time and a filing deadline in hand. And what you are looking for is not always a clear-cut word; more often it is a situation: a tenant being evicted, a contract being declared void because of a particular clause, an administrative act being annulled on procedural grounds.

Traditional search engines, however, think in words, not in situations. You want to describe a set of facts; the system expects you to type the exact legal term that corresponds to them. At a moment when you have not yet fully pinned down the legal character of the facts -- which is often the very point of the research -- knowing the right term is impossible. The result: hours of scanning and, despite it all, a nagging unease of "did I miss something?"

The real challenge is not finding the word; it is finding the meaning.

The blind spots of keyword search

Classic search relies on exact word matching. It matches the words in your query against an index and ranks documents using methods such as TF-IDF or BM25. This approach is fast and works flawlessly when you are searching for a clearly named statutory provision. The trouble begins when the user does not know the exact term; at that point the relevant document simply stays invisible.

Legal language is especially difficult when it comes to synonymy and the variety of terms. Consider a few examples:

  • "Eviction on grounds of need" (ihtiyaç nedeniyle tahliye) versus "eviction by reason of requirement" (gereksinim sebebiyle tahliye): the same legal institution, different words.
  • Searching for "eviction" (tahliye) while the decision uses the phrase "vacating the leased property" (kiralananın boşaltılması).
  • "Car repair" versus "automotive maintenance": an everyday example, but its logic maps directly onto law; different words, almost the same meaning.

Keyword search cannot see these affinities. To it, "need" (ihtiyaç) and "requirement" (gereksinim) are two entirely foreign strings. The second blind spot is even subtler: being unable to search without already knowing the legal character of the facts. If you cannot translate the concrete facts your client describes into the right legal concept, you cannot know which keyword to type either. The search stalls before it even begins.

How does semantic search work?

Semantic search has one fundamental difference: it matches not the word but the meaning. Even though "car repair" and "automotive maintenance" are made of different words, they reach the same results because they are close in meaning. So how does a machine "grasp" meaning? The answer lies in an idea that sounds technical but is actually quite intuitive: the embedding.

An embedding is a numerical representation of a text's meaning. A sentence or paragraph is converted into sequences of numbers that resemble coordinates in a high-dimensional space, that is, into vectors. In this space, things that are similar in meaning sit close to one another, while unrelated things sit far apart. You can picture it as a vast library: books are no longer shelved by the alphabet but by their subject. Everything to do with "leases" gathers in the same corner; whatever word you ask with, you are taken to that corner.

How close two texts are to each other is most often measured with cosine similarity (comparing the direction between vectors on a scale from -1 to 1) or with the k-nearest neighbors (kNN) algorithm. All these vectors are stored in vector databases, so that the closest in meaning among millions of documents can be retrieved within seconds.

Tip: For legal texts, embedding models trained specifically on a legal corpus, such as LEGAL-BERT, capture terminological nuance and the structure of decisions better than general-purpose models. Decisions are first split into meaningful chunks, then each chunk is vectorized separately; this way, not a single word but an entire line of reasoning becomes searchable by its meaning.

The sheer volume of Turkish case law

To understand why this technology is not a luxury but a necessity, it is enough to look at the scale of the Turkish judiciary. The numbers alone demolish any idea of scanning case law one by one with the human eye.

These are only a cross-section. The number of files concluded by the Court of Cassation (Yargıtay) in 2023 has surpassed 264 thousand; around 110 thousand individual applications were made to the Constitutional Court in the same year. The Council of State's (Danıştay) official precedent search system alone holds more than 379 thousand documents. Add to that the rapidly growing appellate corpus of the Regional Courts of Justice (BAM). The picture is clear: no jurist can find the most fitting precedent by scanning this volume page by page.

The good news is that the official sources are accessible: systems such as UYAP Precedent Search, the Court of Cassation (Yargıtay) Decision Search, and the Council of State (Danıştay) Precedent Search are open to everyone. But being accessible and being accessible by meaning are different things. The real challenge is being able to pull the right decision out of this vast pool with the right question.

Describe the facts in your own words, find the precedent

This is the most tangible benefit of semantic search for a jurist: you can now describe the facts in your own words. You can feed the system an everyday, natural sentence such as "The tenant gave an undertaking to vacate, but the date was left blank; is an eviction based on the undertaking possible?" without having to know the exact technical term it corresponds to. The system grasps the meaning of your sentence and takes you to decisions that carry the same legal core.

A good legal search layer goes one step further here: it highlights the actual reasoning of the decision it brings up, that is, the essence of the decision (ratio decidendi). Instead of getting lost across dozens of pages, it puts the answer to "why was this decision rendered the way it was?" right in front of you. What is more, it automatically matches the decision with the relevant legislation; which statutory provision, which communiqué, which regulation is in play, you see them all together.

Summary + legislation matching

This is where the logic of RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) comes in. The system first brings up the semantically relevant decision, then generates the answer grounded in that actual document. In this way, a plain language model's problem of "being unable to access the real document" is largely overcome; the summary is not pulled out of thin air but born from the decision in your hand.

This approach links the three pillars of law -- the triangle of legislation, case law, and communiqués -- in a single flow. It is worth recalling here the hierarchy of sources in Turkish law: there is a clear order running through the Constitution, statutes, decree-laws, presidential decrees, bylaws, regulations, and communiqués. Case law, as a rule, is not a binding norm but an interpretation of the norm; the exceptions are Decisions on the Unification of Case Law and Constitutional Court decisions. A tool that knows this distinction presents the precedent it brings with the right weight; it does not put a chamber decision of the Court of Cassation (Yargıtay) on the same footing as a Decision on the Unification of Case Law.

Key takeaways

  • Keyword search matches the exact word, while semantic search matches the meaning; only the latter catches synonymous legal terms.
  • An embedding turns text into a vector in a meaning space; similarity is measured with cosine similarity or kNN.
  • The volume of Turkish case law (hundreds of thousands of decisions) is too large to scan with the human eye.
  • Describing the facts in natural language and seeing the essence of the decision together with the relevant legislation is the greatest practical gain.
  • The most fitting result is born from a hybrid approach that combines semantic and keyword search.

Limits and responsibility

Here we must be honest: AI does not replace the jurist's judgment. Language models carry a well-known risk: hallucination, that is, producing wrong or entirely nonexistent information with an air of confidence. This is not an abstract concern. An assessment by Stanford researchers revealed that even leading commercial legal tools marketed with claims of "no hallucinations" (such as Lexis+ AI and Westlaw AI-Assisted Research) can produce wrong or fabricated information between 17% and 33% of the time.

RAG markedly lowers this rate, because it ties the model to real documents. But it does not eliminate it entirely. Therefore, a few principles are non-negotiable:

  • Always verify the source. Confirm the number, chamber, and date of the retrieved decision against the original text.
  • Check whether it is current. The relevant statute may be repealed (no longer in force); provisional articles or a later line of case law may have changed the picture.
  • Use hybrid search. Find by meaning first, then narrow down and confirm with keywords and filters.

If you are curious to dig deeper into how AI turns from a tool that "makes things up" into one that "cites its sources," the posts what is RAG and what is a large language model are a good place to start.

Is semantic search replacing keyword search?

No. The two complement each other. When searching for a clear statutory provision or a decision number, keyword search is stronger; when searching for a set of facts by its meaning, semantic search comes to the fore. The most robust result comes from a hybrid search that combines the two.

What exactly is an embedding?

An embedding is a numerical representation of a text's meaning. The text is converted into a sequence of numbers (a vector) that resembles coordinates in a high-dimensional space. Texts that are similar in meaning sit close to one another in this space; the search works by measuring that closeness.

Can I trust the decision the AI brings up?

As a starting point, yes; as a definitive result, no. Because language models can produce wrong information, it is essential to verify the source against the original text and to check whether the legislation is current. The tool speeds up the research; the final responsibility rests with the jurist.

Is case law a binding norm?

As a rule, no; case law is an interpretation of the norm and is not considered a binding norm. The main exceptions to this are Decisions on the Unification of Case Law and Constitutional Court decisions. A good search tool presents its results taking this difference in weight into account.

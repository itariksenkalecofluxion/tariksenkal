Trustworthy AI in law: how do we prevent hallucination?

TŞ
İsmail Tarık Şenkal
EcoFluxion · Co-Founder
· 9 min read

The most insidious flaw of artificial intelligence is its inability to say it doesn't know. When a large language model cannot find an answer, it usually doesn't stay silent; on the contrary, it "makes something up" in flawless prose and with a tone of utter confidence. We call this hallucination. In most fields it is an annoying defect. In law, it is a professional catastrophe.

"Confident but wrong": what is hallucination?

A large language model (LLM) is not really a "knowledge base"; it is a statistical engine that predicts what the next word should be by looking at the patterns it has learned from enormous piles of text. That is why it writes so fluently. But for the very same reason it has no ability to verify the truth: when you ask it for the number of a Court of Cassation (Yargıtay) decision, it does not recall a real decision, it merely generates "what a real decision number ought to look like."

That is precisely what hallucination is: the model producing content that does not actually exist yet is coherent enough to seem convincing. In a legal context, hallucination typically appears in three forms:

  • Citing a non-existent case: fabricating a case that was never filed, complete with name, number and date.
  • A fake quotation from a real case: attributing to an existing decision a sentence that never appears in it.
  • False support: a genuine quotation that does not actually support the legal proposition being advanced, or even contradicts it.
The danger lies not in the model being wrong, but in its being persuasive enough to conceal that it is wrong.

A cautionary case: Mata v. Avianca and six fake precedents

The event that explained this problem to the world most clearly is the Mata v. Avianca case, heard in 2023 before the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York (case no. 1:22-cv-01461). The plaintiff's attorneys used ChatGPT while preparing a brief. The brief they submitted cited six separate precedent decisions. The problem was this: none of these decisions were real; all six were entirely fabricated.

In his explanation to the court, one of the attorneys said he had treated ChatGPT almost like a "super search engine," assuming the system would only return real decisions. By the time the truth came out, it was too late: when the opposing party could not find the decisions cited in the brief in any database, the matter was brought before the judge.

In his ruling of 22 June 2023, Judge P. Kevin Castel found that the attorneys had acted in bad faith. He imposed a monetary sanction under Rule 11 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and required the attorneys to send corrective letters to the real judges who had been listed as authors of the fake decisions.

The real lesson: the source of the error was not the AI, but blind reliance on it. The attorneys never once verified the output against an independent source. The hallucination was inevitable; skipping verification, however, was a choice.

Not an isolated accident, but an epidemic

We could easily have dismissed Mata v. Avianca as "the unfortunate mistake of two inexperienced attorneys." Unfortunately, subsequent developments showed that this was not an exception but the first major sign of a rising trend. In mid-2025, legal researcher Damien Charlotin made public a global database, spanning many countries, that compiles court decisions involving AI hallucinations.

The trajectory of the number of cases added to this database is striking in itself: while two or three cases were being added per day in the autumn of 2025, toward the end of the year this rose to five or six cases per day, with the total record reaching a figure expressed in the hundreds. In other words, the problem did not subside; on the contrary, it accelerated as AI tools became more widespread. This is proof that the issue has ceased to be a matter of individual carelessness and turned into a structural risk for the profession.

Why is it intolerable in law?

A made-up sentence in a marketing text can be corrected, causing embarrassment at most. In law, however, every piece of information submitted to the court is a commitment. Here three fundamental duties come into play:

  • Accuracy: the truthfulness of content entering the file is not negotiable; a single fake citation destroys the credibility of the entire brief.
  • The duty of candor: a lawyer has a duty of candor toward the court; submitting information whose accuracy has not been checked undermines this duty.
  • Client trust: the client entrusts the fate of their life or their business to the lawyer. This trust is incompatible with copying an AI output into the file unchecked.

Moreover, this responsibility cannot be delegated. Even if the misuse is unintentional, the person responsible for the accuracy of the content submitted to the file is the lawyer, not the tool. The American Bar Association (ABA) underscored this principle when it published its first formal ethics opinion on the use of generative AI in July 2024: the tool changed, but the owner of the responsibility did not.

The myth that "law-specific tools don't hallucinate"

Many legal professionals reasonably think: "General-purpose chatbots may make things up, but law-specific commercial tools fed with real case law do not." This reassuring assumption is, unfortunately, not entirely correct.

A comprehensive study conducted by Stanford University's RegLab and HAI teams and published in the Journal of Empirical Legal Studies in 2025 put the industry's leading tools under the microscope. The result was sobering: even these source-based (RAG-based) commercial tools still produced hallucinations in a significant share of queries. The study's headline was that legal research models hallucinated in at least one of every six queries.

The "law-specific" label is not a guarantee of accuracy, but a step taken in the right direction. That is why expert oversight cannot be negotiable, even with the most advanced tool.

The solution architecture: grounding, sourcing and verification

The way to manage hallucination runs not through a single magic button, but through overlapping layers of defense. The foundation of this architecture is RAG (retrieval-augmented generation).

The logic of RAG is simple: the model does not write the answer directly "from memory." It first retrieves the relevant text from a real source (legislation, case law, or a document uploaded by the user), then produces its answer by grounding it in that text. In this way the output rests not on the model's imagination but on the real document placed before it. There is a critical condition to this: the retrieval step itself must be accurate; if the wrong document is retrieved, the answer built on top of it will also be wrong.

Grounding + citation

In a well-designed legal tool, every answer is linked to the real decision or legislative provision it relies on. With a single click, the user can go to the source and verify the text with their own eyes. The ability to answer the question "Where did you find this?" instantly is the strongest practical shield against hallucination.

In this architecture, three layers work together:

  1. Grounding in sources (RAG): the answer rests on the retrieved real document, not on the model's guess.
  2. Citation: every claim is presented with a traceable reference; the user sees the source directly.
  3. The verification layer: the system checks whether the cited reference actually exists and whether it supports the proposition.

Human oversight: the lawyer is always the one ultimately responsible

No matter how much we advance the technology, one fact does not change: AI is not a "super search engine." The fundamental mistake of the attorney in Mata v. Avianca was exactly this; he saw the tool as something it could never be. The correct mental model is this: AI is like an intern who is very fast but sometimes wrong. You do not sign off on its work without reviewing it.

That is why even the most robust architecture "keeps the human in the loop" (human-in-the-loop). The tool provides speed and breadth; the one who assumes the final legal judgment, the context and the responsibility is the lawyer. At EcoFluxion, we design our products with this philosophy too: our aim is not to replace the lawyer, but to offer them a foundation that is fast and in which the source is visible and auditable.

The Turkish context: the TBB, bar associations and the ethical framework

This debate is not abstract for Turkey. Because general-purpose models largely "do not know" Turkish law, they are especially prone to fabricating Court of Cassation (Yargıtay) decisions, legislative provisions and their numbers. This brings to the fore tools specific to Turkish law that are fed with real case law and legislation.

On the institutional front, there is movement too:

  • The Union of Turkish Bar Associations (TBB): an "AI and Law R&D Unit" was established within it. At the "AI and the Legal Profession Workshop" held on 14-15 February 2026, topics such as legislation and rules of the profession, AI literacy, client rights, accreditation and oversight, and the legal technology ecosystem were addressed.
  • Bar association guides: the Ankara Bar Association published its "Guide to the Use of AI Tools in the Legal Profession." The guide is a reminder that the duties of confidentiality, diligence, personal performance and accountability set out in Attorneys' Act No. 1136 apply equally to the use of AI.
  • The European framework: CEPEJ, the Council of Europe's commission for the efficiency of justice, set out five principles in its "Ethical Charter on the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Judicial Systems" of December 2018: respect for fundamental rights, non-discrimination, quality and security, transparency-impartiality-fairness, and user control. Turkey is a member of the Council of Europe, and this framework is of guiding significance.
Tip: When evaluating a tool, don't only ask "how smart is it?"; ask "does it show its source, how does it protect productivity, and which legislation and case law is it fed with?" The right questions filter out hallucination risk as early as the contracting stage.

A practical checklist

Whichever tool you use, a simple discipline to apply before putting the output into the file prevents most disasters from the outset:

  1. Verify every citation against its source. Open the cited decision or provision yourself in an official or trustworthy database; check whether the number, parties and date match.
  2. Confirm that the quotation appears in the text. Is the sentence attributed to the decision really inside that decision?
  3. Question the support. Does the citation, even if genuine, really support the legal proposition you are advancing, or is it only superficially related to the topic?
  4. Check that it is current. The decision may have been overturned, the case law may have changed, or the legislation may have been repealed.
  5. Protect confidentiality. Do not enter client secrets and personal data into insecure general-purpose tools; know the data-processing terms.
  6. Take responsibility. You are the one who signs; "the AI said so" is not a defense.

Key takeaways

  • Hallucination is not a defect of the model but a natural consequence of how it works; it is managed by design, not eliminated by wishing.
  • Mata v. Avianca showed that the real risk is not AI, but trust without verification.
  • The Stanford findings revealed that even "law-specific" tools are not entirely immune to hallucination.
  • The solution is a stack: grounding in sources (RAG) + citation + verification + human oversight.
  • In Turkey, the framework of the TBB, the bar associations and CEPEJ is laying the groundwork for responsible use.
A tool that uses RAG doesn't hallucinate, right?

No. By grounding the answer in a real source, RAG greatly reduces hallucination, but does not eliminate it entirely. Stanford's 2025 study showed that even leading RAG-based commercial tools can hallucinate on a significant share of queries. That is why human oversight is essential.

If I put a fake precedent into the file, who bears the responsibility?

The lawyer. Even if the misuse is unintentional, the person responsible for the accuracy of the content submitted to the court is the lawyer. "The AI made it up" is not an excuse; in Mata v. Avianca the attorneys faced sanctions for precisely this reason.

Can I use general-purpose chatbots for Turkish law?

It is not advisable without great care. Because general-purpose models largely "do not know" Turkish legislation and case law, they are prone to fabricating article numbers and decisions. Tools specific to Turkish law that are fed with real case law and legislation and that cite their sources reduce this risk markedly.

What should I look for when choosing a legal AI tool?

Citation and traceability (is every answer linked to a real decision or provision?), the scope and currency of the data source it is fed with, data security and KVKK compliance, and the room it leaves for user control. There is no tool that guarantees accuracy; there are tools that make verification easier.

Conclusion: Trust = Accuracy + Traceability

Trust in AI in law is not measured by how "smart" the model appears. It is measured by two things: whether what it says is accurate, and whether that accuracy is traceable. An answer that cannot show its source, however fluent, cannot bear a lawyer's signature.
